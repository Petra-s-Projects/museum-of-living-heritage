Livingstone Museum

Zambia's oldest and largest museum

The Livingstone Museum is not just a repository — it is the institution where Zambian history was written. The scholars who worked here transformed how Africa's past was understood, replacing colonial assumptions with rigorous archaeology and pioneering African social anthropology.

The Story

In the basement of Zambia's oldest museum, cardboard boxes overflow with documents that would make any historian weep: complete runs of Parliamentary Debates stretching back to the Legislative Council of Northern Rhodesia, stacks of The Northern News with front pages announcing Kaunda's showdowns with unfriendly countries, administrative files from district commissioners who ruled territories larger than English counties. Upstairs, in glass cases, lie letters bearing David Livingstone's signature, copper ingots from the medieval trading post at Ingombe Ilede, and ethnographic collections assembled when the peoples described were living memory rather than historical record.

The story begins in 1934, when a small collection of Livingstone memorabilia was assembled to commemorate the centenary of the explorer's arrival in Africa. The David Livingstone Memorial Museum, as it was first known, occupied modest premises in the town that bore his name — Livingstone having served as the capital of Northern Rhodesia until 1935.

In 1937, the museum was renamed the Rhodes-Livingstone Museum, and the same year saw the establishment of the Rhodes-Livingstone Institute — an affiliated research body that would become internationally famous for pioneering work in social anthropology. Together, they transformed Livingstone into an unlikely centre of African scholarship.

The appointment of J. Desmond Clark as Curator in 1938 marked a turning point. Clark would spend the next twenty-three years at the museum, building it into one of Africa's most important archaeological research institutions. His excavations at Kalambo Falls revealed one of the longest archaeological sequences in Africa — evidence of human occupation spanning over 300,000 years. When Clark left in 1961 to take up a professorship at Berkeley, he was recognised as one of the founders of modern African archaeology.

At independence in 1964, the museum was renamed simply "The Livingstone Museum" — dropping "Rhodes" from its title in a symbolic break with the colonial past. Today, it holds over 25,000 objects spanning archaeology, ethnography, natural history, and social history.

What You Will See

The Livingstone Gallery occupies the heart of the museum. Here, in wooden display cases that themselves have historical character, are the letters and personal effects of David Livingstone. A cabinet displays correspondence written in Livingstone's distinctive hand — look closely and you can read his sign-off: "I am your Most obedient Servant, David Livingstone." Wall panels document his major expeditions: the trans-African journey of 1853–56, the Zambezi Expedition of 1858–64, and the final journeys of 1865–73 that ended with his death at Chitambo's village.

The Archaeology Gallery displays the museum's extraordinary prehistoric collections. Stone tools from Kalambo Falls document human technological evolution from the Acheulean hand-axes of half a million years ago to the microlithic industries of the Later Stone Age. A highlight is the material from Ingombe Ilede, the Iron Age trading settlement excavated in 1960–62. The site yielded spectacular burial goods: copper ingots, wire bangles, glass beads from the Indian Ocean trade, and gold ornaments demonstrating medieval trading networks spanning half a continent.

The Ethnography Gallery holds collections assembled in the colonial period — objects that colonial administrators collected as curiosities are now invaluable records of material cultures that have since transformed. Look for the Akabende — a small decorated mortar used in girls' initiation ceremonies.

The History Gallery traces Zambia's journey from pre-colonial times through colonialism to independence. A vintage Volkswagen Beetle, positioned against a painted backdrop of 1980s Lusaka, captures everyday urban life. Display panels document the independence struggle.

The Art Collection includes works by early Zambian modernists, including a striking painting by Ranford Sililo dated 1 October 1951, depicting scenes of African life at a moment of transformation.

The Archives — available to researchers by arrangement — constitute one of the museum's most significant holdings, including Parliamentary Debates, Hansard volumes, and newspaper runs essential for research on Zambian history.

The Building

Don't miss the building itself. The arched doorways reflect the Mediterranean Revival style favoured by colonial architects in the 1930s–50s. The clock tower, added in 1951 and donated by pioneers Harry and Elie Susman, commands the main facade. The interior galleries — with their wooden display cases, terracotta floor tiles, and high ceilings — retain the atmosphere of a mid-century museum.

Getting There

The museum is located on Mosi-oa-Tunya Road in central Livingstone, approximately 500 metres north of the main post office and 1.5 kilometres from the border crossing to Zimbabwe. The distinctive clock tower marks the entrance.

By taxi: Any Livingstone taxi driver will know the museum. Expect to pay K30–50 from hotels in the tourist area.

On foot: Walkable from central Livingstone and from hotels along Mosi-oa-Tunya Road.

Tips for Visitors

  • Start with the Livingstone Gallery: The explorer's letters and personal effects provide compelling entry points.
  • Look beyond the labels: Colonial-era interpretations sometimes jar; the objects themselves tell richer stories.
  • Ask about the archives: Researchers can arrange access to newspaper collections and parliamentary records.
  • Engage with staff: The museum's guides have deep knowledge and personal connections to the collections.
  • Note the building: The Mediterranean Revival architecture is itself historically significant.
  • Allow time: The collections reward unhurried exploration; allow 2–3 hours minimum.
