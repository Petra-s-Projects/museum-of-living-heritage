Old Drift Cemetery

Zambia's first colonial settlement — a fever camp where dreams came to die

Thirty-one settlers. Eleven dead in a single rainy season. The Old Drift was Zambia's first colonial settlement — a fever camp where dreams came to die. All that remains is this small cemetery in the bush, where weathered marble tells the stories of those who travelled halfway around the world and never left.

The Story

In 1898, European settlers established a crossing point on the Zambezi River about ten kilometres upstream from Victoria Falls. The Old Drift — named for the ford across the river — became the gateway to the territories north of the Zambezi, a ramshackle settlement of traders, hunters, and hopefuls seeking fortune in the African interior.

They had chosen badly. The site lay in low, marshy ground — perfect breeding habitat for mosquitoes. Malaria was endemic. Its complication, blackwater fever, was a death sentence. By the rainy season of 1902–03, the settlement was dying. A doctor, John Neil Wilson, arrived from the south and established a clinic. Within three months, Dr Wilson himself was dead. The chemists who came to dispense medicine followed him to the grave. Of thirty-one settlers present that season, eleven died — a mortality rate of thirty-five per cent.

The Graves

The individual headstones tell stories that statistics cannot. Dr John Neil Wilson MB CM arrived at the Drift in October 1902 with Nurse Chapman, and together they established a private clinic. Three months later, the doctor was dead. Nurse Chapman — whose first name history has not recorded — continued to run the small nursing hospital alone until 1905, caring for the sick and watching many of them die. Wilson's marble stone bears an epitaph of heartbreaking beauty: "Your name, your deeds, will be as legible on the hearts you left behind as the stars on the brow of evening."

Samuel Thomas Alexander — the cemetery plaque incorrectly names him "Taylor" — had lived an extraordinary life before he reached the Zambezi. Born to American missionary parents in Hawaii in 1836, he came to the Falls as a tourist in 1904, aged sixty-eight. He died not from fever but from a falling rock dislodged during excavations for the Victoria Falls Bridge. From the Pacific to Africa, his journey ended here beneath a broken marble slab.

Percy C. Wyld was bookkeeper with the Bechuanaland Trading Association. He died on 16 July 1904, aged forty-two. His broken headstone offers no epitaph — just a name, a date, and silence.

Georges Mercier's grave lies a short distance from the main cemetery — a Swiss carpenter, just twenty-six years old, who had come with his wife in 1897 to help build the Paris Missionary Society station at Sefula in Barotseland. He fell ill at the Drift in late 1900, on his way home to Europe. He never left.

Not all the dead have marked graves. The cemetery plaque records "fourteen others whose names are not known." One, a Mr Southurst, disappeared into the bush during a malarial fever and was found dead some time later.

The End of the Old Drift

The railway from Bulawayo reached the Victoria Falls in mid-1904. The following year, the Victoria Falls Bridge opened, crossing the gorge below the Falls and rendering the Old Drift crossing obsolete. The surviving settlers moved to higher ground ten kilometres north, founding the town that would become Livingstone.

The Old Drift was abandoned. The bush reclaimed the buildings. Today, nothing remains of the settlement itself — only this small cemetery, enclosed within Mosi-oa-Tunya National Park, where elephants browse among the graves and the dead keep their vigil over the river they came to cross.

Historical Significance

The Old Drift holds a unique place in Zambian history as the first European settlement in what would become Northern Rhodesia. Its failure — the fever, the deaths, the eventual abandonment — shaped the development of the region. Livingstone exists where it does because the Old Drift proved uninhabitable.

The cemetery also serves as a reminder of the realities behind colonial expansion. The settlers who came to the Zambezi were not abstractions but individuals: a Swiss carpenter aged twenty-six, a Scottish doctor aged forty-five, an American born in Hawaii who died beneath African rock. Their stories complicate simple narratives of conquest and exploitation. They do not excuse the injustices of empire, but they add human texture to the historical record.

What You Will See

This is a contemplative site, not a spectacle. The cemetery is small — a handful of graves in a clearing, some headstones broken, inscriptions weathering into illegibility. The power lies in what the place represents: the human cost of empire, the fragility of life on the frontier, the stories of individuals who became statistics.

Take time to read the inscriptions. Consider the distances these people travelled, the hopes they carried, the ends they met. Whatever one thinks of colonialism — and there is much to condemn — these were human beings who deserve to be remembered.

Getting There

The cemetery lies within Mosi-oa-Tunya National Park, accessible from the main park gate near Livingstone. You will need to pay park entry fees and ideally arrange a guide — the cemetery is not prominently signposted and the park contains wildlife including elephants, buffalo, and occasionally lion.

Ask at the gate for directions to the Old Drift Cemetery. Most visitors combine the cemetery with a game drive through the park.
