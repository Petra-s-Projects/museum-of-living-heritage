Chambeshi Monument

Where the First World War finally ended — three days after the Armistice

The last shot of the First World War was not fired in France. It was fired here, in the Zambian bush, three days after the Armistice. A stone pillar, a rusting cannon, and the ruins of a sabotaged bridge mark where two wars ended — sixty years apart.

The Story

At 7.30am on Thursday 14 November 1918, a small group of men met on the north bank of the Chambeshi River. One was General Paul von Lettow-Vorbeck, commander of German forces in East Africa, leading an undefeated army that had eluded British pursuit for four years across Tanzania, Mozambique, and now Northern Rhodesia. The other was Hector Croad, District Commissioner of Kasama, carrying a telegram.

The message was simple: the war was over. An Armistice had been signed in Europe three days earlier. Von Lettow-Vorbeck was to cease hostilities immediately.

The German general had led one of military history's most remarkable guerrilla campaigns. With never more than 14,000 men — mostly African askaris — he had tied down over 300,000 British and Commonwealth troops, raiding supply lines, disrupting communications, and refusing to be cornered. When the Armistice was signed in Europe, von Lettow-Vorbeck was advancing on Kasama, having captured the town's supplies the previous day.

The tiny British population had fled to Mpika; nine men remained at the Chambeshi with two Maxim guns they didn't know how to operate. One of them, Charlie Simpson, had buried £10,000 in cash — the entire contents of Kasama's government offices and businesses — in a goat pen at his rubber factory, reasoning that the Germans would be more interested in the goats than in looking for buried treasure.

The Germans machine-gunned the rubber factory anyway, just before Croad arrived with the telegram.

Von Lettow-Vorbeck agreed to a ceasefire on the spot. He then marched his undefeated troops 250km north to Abercorn (now Mbala) for the formal surrender on 25 November — a date Zambia still commemorates. His remaining force: thirty German officers, 125 other German ranks, 1,168 askaris, and 3,500 porters. They had never lost a battle.

The monument was unveiled on 14 November 1953 — the 35th anniversary — by L.W.G. Eccles, a retired surveyor who had served with the Northern Rhodesia Rifles during the campaign. It consists of a stone pillar bearing two plaques: one in English recording the event, one in Bemba honouring "all brave soldiers who fought in this war" (Twapela umuchinshi kuli bonse abashipa abalwile mu nkondo iyi). Beside it stands an 1890 breech-loading field gun similar to those used by German forces.

Two Bridges, Two Wars

The monument stands near the ruins of the old Chambeshi road bridge — but this bridge has its own story, from a different war entirely.

During the Rhodesian Bush War of the 1970s, Zambia provided sanctuary for Zimbabwean liberation movements. In 1979, Rhodesian forces mounted coordinated attacks on Zambian infrastructure, destroying bridges across the country's south. The old Chambeshi road bridge was among the casualties — its shattered pylons still stand as silent witnesses to that later conflict.

The TAZARA railway bridge, visible in the background, was bombed on 11 October 1979. The explosives detonated just minutes after a southbound passenger train had crossed. The driver was Evelyn Mwansa, Zambia's first female locomotive driver. She later said she had sensed something was wrong and increased speed across the bridge — possibly saving hundreds of lives.

A new road bridge now carries the Great North Road across the Chambeshi, but the ruins of the old bridge remain, adding an unexpected layer to a site already rich in history.

Living Memory

The Chambeshi Monument is not a forgotten relic. On 11 November 2018 — the centenary of the European Armistice — a commemoration brought together veterans from Zambia and the United Kingdom, along with a descendant of von Lettow-Vorbeck himself. The site remains well-maintained, with a caretaker who will guide visitors to the monument and share its history.

What You Will See

The monument itself is modest: a stone pillar perhaps two metres tall, the plaques weathered but legible, the NHCC emblem mounted below. Beside it, the 1890 field gun — painted white, showing its age in patches of rust — points across the bush as if still awaiting an enemy that never came.

The setting is what gives the site its power. This is not a manicured war cemetery or a grand memorial arch. It is a clearing in the African bush, the Chambeshi River flowing nearby, the ruins of a later war's destruction visible a short walk away. The Great North Road rumbles past. Most travellers pass without stopping.

Those who do stop find themselves at a genuine historical crossroads: the place where the First World War ended, where the Rhodesian Bush War left its scars, and where — once a century — people still gather to remember.

Getting There

The monument is located on the Great North Road (T2), approximately 100km south of Kasama and 3km north of the Chambeshi River bridge. When heading north, watch for a sign on the left shortly after crossing the river. A short track leads to the monument; the caretaker will come to meet you.

Tips

  • Combine with Kasama sites: The monument is a natural stop when travelling between Mpika and Kasama. Continue north to Mwela Rocks and Chishimba Falls.
  • Allow time to explore: The ruined bridge pylons are a short walk from the monument and worth seeing.
  • Bring the story: This site rewards visitors who arrive knowing the history. Share what you've learned with the caretaker — he may have details to add.
