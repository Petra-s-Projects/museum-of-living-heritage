Katolola Rock Art Site

Prehistoric paintings of Eastern Zambia

Ladder designs and a giant eland painted in purple: at this granite kopje near Chipata, two ancient artistic traditions overlap in ways found nowhere else in Zambia. The schematic grids came first, then Late Stone Age hunters returned to paint a massive antelope right over the top — proof that these rock faces held meaning across millennia.

The Story

Most Zambian rock art sites raise questions that can never be answered. Who were the painters? What did the images mean? When were they made? Katolola offers something rare: evidence that helps answer at least one of these mysteries.

The site lies at the foot of Katolola Hills, a dramatic granite kopje rising from the bush east of Chipata. Two painted rock-shelters contain work in strikingly different styles. The first, little more than an overhang, bears a remarkable naturalistic painting of an eland — almost two metres long, sketched in thin purple lines. The dewlap and mane are clearly visible, though the legs are disproportionately small and weathering has erased the head.

What makes this painting significant is what lies beneath it: a large schematic grid, now partially obscured by the eland painted over the top. This superimposition matters. Across Central Africa, archaeologists have debated which painting tradition came first — the geometric schematic designs or the naturalistic animal figures. Katolola demonstrates the opposite sequence at this particular site: here, the schematic grid was painted first, and the naturalistic eland came later. The site thus provides crucial evidence for chronological overlap between the two traditions.

The Second Shelter

A second rock face lies a short distance to the north-east. Here the paintings are entirely schematic: large and carefully executed groups of concentric circles, grids, and the distinctive "ladder" motifs that appear across central Zambian rock art sites. From one grid emerge two horizontal ladder designs, with dots of red paint below forming a pattern that resembles rain falling from a cloud.

This imagery is significant. The rock face is pitted with ancient scars where stones have been thrown at the paintings — damage that patination shows occurred long ago. Evidence from other sites suggests this stone-throwing formed part of traditional rain-making ceremonies, and the "rain from cloud" design at Katolola supports this interpretation.

The Spiritual Eland

The eland itself carries spiritual weight. In southern African San rock art, the eland held deep ritual significance — it was the animal most commonly depicted and most closely associated with trance states and spiritual power. The appearance of an eland at Katolola, executed with evident care despite its weathered condition, suggests that this Eastern Province site may share symbolic connections with rock art traditions stretching far to the south.

What You Will See

The approach to Katolola takes you through typical Eastern Province bush along the Chipata-Chadiza road. The granite kopje is visible from some distance — a distinctive outcrop rising above the surrounding woodland.

The two painted shelters lie at the base of the western side of the kopje. The first is essentially an overhang rather than a true cave, with the great eland painted on the protected rock face. Look carefully at the area around the eland's body — with patience, you can make out the underlying schematic grid that the naturalistic figure was painted over. The purple-brown pigment of the eland contrasts with the red ochre of the geometric design beneath.

The second shelter, a short scramble to the north-east, contains the more elaborate schematic paintings. The ladder motifs are immediately striking — bold parallel lines with regular cross-bars, executed in red ochre that remains remarkably vivid. Note the pitting on the rock surface around the paintings; these are the ancient scars from stone-throwing, physical evidence of the ceremonies that once took place here.

Getting There

From Chipata, head east on the road towards Chadiza. The kopje is approximately 14km from town, visible on the south side of the road. There is no formal signage or marked turnoff — look for the distinctive granite outcrop and find a place to pull off the road.

The walk from the road to the painted shelters is short but involves some scrambling over rocks. No special equipment is needed, but sturdy footwear is advisable.

Tips for Visitors

  • Visit in morning or late afternoon for the best light on the painted surfaces
  • Allow time for your eyes to adjust — the schematic designs become clearer the longer you look
  • Examine the rock surface around the paintings for ancient pitting from stone-throwing
  • Never touch the paintings — oils from skin accelerate deterioration
  • The second shelter with the ladder motifs is easy to miss; look to the north-east after viewing the eland
  • Bring water — there are no facilities at the site

Suggested Itineraries

Chipata Rock Art Circuit (half-day): Katolola in the morning, Zawi Hill in the afternoon. Both sites are unmanaged but accessible, offering contrasting examples of Eastern Province rock art.

Eastern Province Heritage (2–3 days): Day 1: Chipata, Katolola → Day 2: Zawi Hill, drive to South Luangwa → Day 3: South Luangwa National Park. Combines rock art with wildlife.
