Cairo Road Heritage Walk

An open-air archive of everything Zambia has been

The name is a colonial relic — Cecil Rhodes' fantasy of a Cape-to-Cairo corridor through British Africa. But walk Cairo Road today and you encounter something more complex: a 1.8-kilometre timeline of Zambian ambition, from the oldest surviving building in the capital to the tallest. Here stands Lusaka's only surviving evidence of the colonial colour bar — a sealed window where Black Africans once bought railway tickets from outside while whites walked in.

The Story

Cairo Road began as a stretch of dirt in the 1930s, part of the planned new capital that Governor Hubert Young was carving from the bush. Lusaka had been chosen to replace Livingstone in 1935 — more central, better positioned at the crossroads of the Great North Road and Great East Road, with the railway running through. The street took its name from Rhodes' never-completed vision: a road and railway linking Cape Town to Cairo through unbroken British territory.

The early buildings were modest: single-storey shops, the railway station, administrative offices funded by the Beit Trust. The Njanji Commuter Building, constructed in 1906 as a station house, predates even the formal founding of Lusaka as capital — making it the oldest surviving structure in the city. Its architecture encodes the racial hierarchy of colonial Northern Rhodesia: the main entrance for Europeans, a small external hatch for Africans.

The Yugoslav Towers

Independence in 1964 brought new ambitions. Kenneth Kaunda's Zambia was rich with copper revenue and politically positioned as a leader of the Non-Aligned Movement. Yugoslavia, a founding NAM member under Tito, exported modernist architecture across Africa as diplomatic soft power. Zambia became a major recipient.

Between 1970 and 1979, Yugoslav architects designed at least three major buildings in Lusaka: the Mulungushi International Conference Centre (built in 107 days for the 1970 NAM Summit), ZANACO House (headquarters of the national bank), and most dramatically, Findeco House — the 23-storey tower that remains Zambia's tallest building.

Designed by Dušan Milenković and Branimir Ganović, Findeco's floors cantilever off a central pillar, giving it a gravity-defying appearance that symbolised the optimism of the copper boom years. That optimism didn't last. Copper prices collapsed in the late 1970s; the towers remained but maintenance faltered.

The Buildings

Njanji Commuter Building (1906) — The oldest surviving building in Lusaka. Look for the sealed segregation window — a rectangular outline in the brickwork where Black Africans bought tickets from outside. The colour bar made visible in brick and mortar.

Findeco House (1977–1979) — Zambia's tallest building at 90 metres / 23 floors. Yugoslav brutalist design with floors cantilevered off a central pillar. The gold-toned façade, though weathered, remains striking. A Samsung sign crowns the rooftop.

ZANACO House (1970s) — The white tower beside Findeco. Yugoslav-designed headquarters for Zambia National Commercial Bank. Clean white façade contrasts with Findeco's brutalist drama.

Society House / Hilton Garden Inn (1970s; rebuilt 2017) — A phoenix from the ashes. Gutted by fire in the late 1990s and derelict for nearly two decades, a $100 million redevelopment brought it back as Zambia's first Hilton property, clad in copper-toned panels.

Indeco House (1970s) — One of the "big four" towers. Built for the Industrial Development Corporation during the nationalisation era.

The Walk

Suggested route: South to North (1.8 km, 90–120 minutes)

Begin at Heroes Square at the southern end where the Njanji Commuter Building faces the railway station. Examine the sealed segregation window. Note the Freedom Statue nearby.

Walk north along Cairo Road's eastern pavement. The street is divided by a tree-lined median — jacarandas and flamboyants that soften the concrete. Pass the Central Post Office and various mid-century commercial buildings.

Approaching the Kafue Roundabout, the Yugoslav twins dominate the view: ZANACO House (white) to the left, Findeco House (gold) to the right. Stand at the roundabout to appreciate Findeco's cantilevered floors.

Continue to Society House / Hilton Garden Inn — the copper-clad resurrection. End at the Kabwe Roundabout at the northern terminus.

Historical Significance

Colonial Foundation: Cairo Road is the physical spine of colonial Lusaka — laid out in the 1930s, named for Rhodes' imperial fantasy. The Njanji Building's segregation window is tangible evidence of the colour bar.

Non-Aligned Architecture: The Yugoslav-designed buildings represent a significant chapter in African architectural history. Yugoslavia's export of modernist design to NAM member states created a distinctive architectural language across the continent.

Copper Boom and Bust: The 1970s towers embody the optimism of Zambia's first decade. The subsequent decades of decay — Findeco's dark stairwells, Society House's burnt shell — tell the story of economic collapse. Recent redevelopments suggest cautious recovery.

Tips

  • Walk in the morning — Cooler and less congested
  • Bring water — No facilities en route
  • Keep valuables secure — This is a busy commercial area
  • Photography is permitted — But be respectful of people
  • Look up — The architectural details reward attention
  • Allow 90–120 minutes — Don't rush the heritage
