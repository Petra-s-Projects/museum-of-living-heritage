Leopards Hill Cave

Stone Age secrets near Lusaka

"One of the most unique caves in Zambia." A spectacular natural cavern system that burrows straight through a kopje, its cathedral-scale chambers draped with stalactites and home to thousands of bats. This is Zambian heritage at its most raw.

The Story

The official records call this an archaeological site of local significance with no visitor interest. The officials are wrong. Leopards Hill Cave is a spectacular natural cavern system that burrows straight through a kopje in the Lusaka countryside, its chambers draped with stalactites and home to a vast colony of bats. The name recalls the early twentieth century, when lions and leopards still roamed this area, preying on settlers' cattle. Today there are no big cats, but there is something equally thrilling: a hole in a hill that opens into cathedral-scale darkness.

The cave earned its place on the National Monuments register for archaeological reasons — and significant ones at that. In 1958, the legendary archaeologist J. Desmond Clark excavated the cave and discovered evidence of human occupation spanning over 60,000 years. The site yielded material from all three phases of the Nachikufan tradition, the Later Stone Age culture that dominated Zambia, making it the southernmost known Nachikufan site. Beneath the cave floor, sealed under a layer of travertine, Clark found an enigmatic "pre-Later Stone Age" deposit that has intrigued researchers ever since.

But the cave's significance extends far beyond archaeology. This is one of the largest natural cave systems in Lusaka Province, formed over millennia as slightly acidic groundwater dissolved the limestone bedrock. The result is a network of chambers and passages that runs entirely through the hill, entering on one side and emerging on the other. The formations inside — stalactites hanging from the ceiling, flowstone cascading down the walls, pillars where ceiling and floor have joined — rival those of better-known caves elsewhere in Zambia.

What You Will See

The cave entrance opens into the side of a limestone kopje, a dark mouth in the pale rock face. From outside, it's easy to underestimate what lies within. Step inside and your eyes adjust to reveal a substantial chamber, high enough to stand in, extending back into darkness.

The cave system runs through the hill, with the main passage connecting entrances on opposite sides. There is one massive main chamber, and smaller side chambers branch off from the main route. The rock surfaces are sculpted by millennia of water action — smooth flowstone, sharp-edged stalactites, and the characteristic scalloped walls of solution caves.

The bats are unavoidable — and it is best to wear a hat! In the main chamber, flying bats fill the air, and the roof can appear alive. The noise is loud, the smell of guano is strong — and there are some extraordinary creepy crawlies on the floor. This is not a sanitised cave experience with neat walkways, it's the real thing!

After passing through the main chamber, there is a climb to the cave exit that brings you out on the other side of the hill — you then walk back around the hill to the entrance.

Getting There

From Lusaka, head east on Leopards Hill Road. The cave is approximately 60 kilometres from the city centre, after crossing two small streams. At the Chirundu/Chongwe junction, take the Chirundu turning. After approximately 300 metres, turn right off the tar road onto an unsigned dirt track.

There are no signposts, but you won't need them — local lads will spot any vehicle coming and will run to meet you, offering to show you the way to the cave entrance. Follow their directions along the track (approximately 3km). You can park directly by the cave entrance. The tracks are passable by ordinary vehicle in dry season; 4x4 may be advisable in the rains.

Local guides: The young men who greet you will guide you to the cave and show you through. This is the expected arrangement — they know the cave intimately and will point out features you'd miss on your own. A tip of K50-100 is appropriate and supports the local community.

Tips for Visitors

  • Torch and headlamp: Essential — do NOT expect to manage with a mobile phone torch as the cave is completely dark beyond the entrance zone. Bring backup batteries.
  • Sturdy footwear: The cave floor is rocky and uneven, and can be slippery with bat guano.
  • Old clothes: You may brush against dirty rock surfaces or guano.
  • Go with a guide: The local lads know the cave and will enhance your visit. It also ensures someone knows you're there.
  • Morning is best: Cooler for the walk, and the bats are generally calmer before the heat of the day.
  • Respect the bats: Keep noise down, avoid shining lights directly at roosting animals.
  • Allow time: The walk from the road, cave exploration, and return takes 2-3 hours minimum.
