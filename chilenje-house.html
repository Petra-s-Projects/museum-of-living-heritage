Chilenje House 394

Birthplace of Zambian independence

"At about 1 a.m. on Monday morning, March 12, 1959, there was a knock on my door... I remember pausing for a second or two to pull my dressing gown cord tight around my waist before opening the door to admit several police officers."
— Kenneth Kaunda, recalling his arrest at House 394

The Story

In February 1960, Kenneth David Kaunda moved into House 394 in Chilenje South, Lusaka. The modest three-room council house — a front room, kitchen, and bedroom — would become the nerve centre of Zambia's independence movement and witness some of the most pivotal moments in the nation's struggle for freedom.

Kaunda had been released from Salisbury Prison just weeks earlier, where he had been detained during the 1959 Emergency. Upon his release, he was banned from returning to the Northern Province where he had been working as the Secretary-General of the Zambia African National Congress (ZANC). Instead, he was restricted to Lusaka — a decision that would prove fateful, as it placed him at the heart of the colonial capital.

On January 31, 1960, while still finding his footing in Lusaka, Kaunda was elected President of the newly formed United National Independence Party (UNIP) at a historic meeting in Kabwata. House 394 immediately became UNIP's headquarters and the operational base for the independence campaign.

The Cha Cha Cha Campaign

From this small house, Kaunda coordinated the famous "Cha Cha Cha" civil disobedience campaign of 1961 — a nationwide movement of non-violent resistance that included strikes, boycotts, and mass demonstrations. The name came from the popular dance of the era, symbolizing that independence would come quickly and with rhythm.

The front room served as the party office where strategy sessions were held, often late into the night. Here, freedom fighters gathered to plan their next moves while Betty Kaunda, KK's wife, kept everyone fed and the household running. The constant stream of visitors, meetings, and political activity transformed this ordinary township house into what was effectively the shadow government of a nation waiting to be born.

"Mama UNIP" - The Land Rover

In the yard stands one of the museum's most treasured artifacts: the Land Rover known as "Mama UNIP." This hardy vehicle carried Kaunda across the length and breadth of the country during the independence campaign, navigating dirt roads and bush tracks to reach villages and towns where he would address crowds and build support for freedom.

The Land Rover became a symbol of the movement itself — sturdy, reliable, and unstoppable. It survives today as a tangible connection to those years of tireless campaigning, still bearing the marks of its many journeys across Zambian soil.

What You'll See

The Front Room: This served as UNIP's first headquarters. Today it displays photographs, documents, and memorabilia from the independence struggle, including images of key meetings held in this very space.

The Kitchen: Where Betty Kaunda prepared countless meals for freedom fighters and visiting dignitaries alike. The simple cooking facilities speak to the humble circumstances from which a nation was born.

The Bedroom: Perhaps the most poignant room — where Kaunda was sleeping when police came to arrest him at 1 a.m. on March 12, 1959. On display is his handwritten account of that night, titled "How I Was Arrested," describing the moment he opened the door to the police officers who would take him to detention.

The Manuscript: Kaunda's original handwritten account of his arrest is one of the museum's most moving documents. In careful script, he describes hearing the knock, pausing to tie his dressing gown, and facing the officers with the calm dignity that would become his hallmark.

The Yard: Home to "Mama UNIP," the Land Rover, and the outdoor spaces where party members would gather for meetings too large for the small house.

Getting There

Chilenje House 394 is located in Chilenje South township, approximately 5km south of Lusaka city centre. From Cairo Road, head south along Chilenje Road, turn right into the township, and follow signs to the museum. The house is well-marked and easily accessible.

Tips for Visitors

  • Request a guided tour to hear the full stories behind each room and artifact
  • Take time to read Kaunda's handwritten arrest account — it brings history viscerally alive
  • The museum is most meaningful if you understand the context of 1959-1964 — consider reading about UNIP and the independence movement beforehand
  • Don't miss "Mama UNIP" in the yard — the Land Rover is easily overlooked but deeply significant
  • The museum is a short drive from central Lusaka and can be combined with visits to other independence-era sites
