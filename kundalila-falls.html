Kundalila Falls

The Crying Dove of Central Zambia

The Kaombe River plunges off the Muchinga Escarpment in a series of cascades, with the Luangwa Valley stretching to the horizon. Kundalila means "crying dove" in Bemba — named for the birds that once gathered here, and perhaps for the sound of falling water echoing through the gorge.

The Falls

The Muchinga Escarpment runs like a spine through central Zambia, separating the high plateau from the Luangwa Valley below. Where the Kaombe River reaches this edge, it doesn't simply drop — it cascades in stages, spreading across the rock face before gathering into a final plunge.

Kundalila is not the highest waterfall in Zambia, nor the widest. But its setting is exceptional. Stand at the top and you look out across the Albertine Rift — the western branch of the great East African Rift system — with the Luangwa Valley hazy in the distance and mountains fading to blue on the horizon. The escarpment drops away at your feet. The falls thread down through it, white against dark rock.

The Legend of the Crying Dove

The name carries a story. "Kundalila" means "crying dove" in Bemba. Local tradition holds that white doves once gathered at the falls in great numbers — until European prospectors, believing the birds' gizzards contained gold dust picked up from the earth, hunted them to extinction. The doves are gone; their name remains.

What You Will See

The visitor experience at Kundalila divides naturally into three parts.

The viewpoint at the top offers the panoramic perspective — the falls disappearing over the lip, the valley spreading below, the escarpment falling away on either side. This alone justifies the visit for those unwilling or unable to descend.

The descent follows a maintained but steep trail down the escarpment face — roughly 800 metres of switchbacks. The path is manageable for anyone reasonably fit, but the climb back up earns its rest stops. Allow 30–45 minutes down, longer returning.

The base rewards the effort. The pool at the foot of the falls offers swimming in an amphitheatre of spray and rock. The vegetation here is lush — a pocket of green fed by constant moisture. The water is clean and cold, the setting theatrical. Two traditional shrines near the main falls mark sites where local leaders and healers still perform rituals; treat these with respect.

When to Visit

The falls are best in the wet season and immediately after (November–May), when the Kaombe runs full and the curtain of water is at its most impressive. Dry season visits (June–October) offer easier trails and clearer weather, but reduced flow.

Getting There

From the Great North Road (T2), turn west at Kanona — signposted "Kundalila Falls National Monument." The junction is approximately 65 km north of the Serenje turn-off, or 180 km southwest of Mpika. From Kanona, follow the gravel road 13–14 km to the falls. The road crosses the TAZARA railway tracks shortly after the junction.

Road condition varies — generally passable in a high-clearance 2WD during dry season, but 4WD recommended after rains.

Tips for Visitors

  • Bring swimwear — the pool at the base is the highlight for many visitors
  • Allow 2–4 hours for the full experience (longer if camping)
  • The climb back up is strenuous — pace yourself and bring water
  • Respect the traditional shrines near the falls
  • Camp overnight to watch the sunset over the escarpment

Suggested Itineraries

Great North Road Break: Kundalila makes an ideal overnight stop for travellers on the Great North Road between Lusaka and the north. Camp at the falls, swim, watch the sunset over the escarpment.

Central Province Circuit (3–4 days): Lusaka → Kasanka NP (bat migration Oct–Dec) → Kundalila Falls → Nsalu Cave → Livingstone Memorial at Chitambo
