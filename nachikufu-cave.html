Nachikufu Cave

15,000 years of human history

Zambia's only known black rock paintings. Three colours of art layered across 15,000 years. A name that entered the international archaeological lexicon — the "Nachikufan" type was defined here. The Bisa still bring offerings to the ancestral spirits they believe protect this limestone kopje. One of Zambia's most important prehistoric sites, and most rewarding to visit.

The Story

Nachikufu Cave came to archaeological attention in 1948, when its rock paintings were first documented. The site's scientific importance became clear through excavations conducted by J. Desmond Clark — who would become one of the founding figures of African prehistory — and later by S. F. Miller in 1948 and 1953. What they found in the cave floor transformed our understanding of Later Stone Age Zambia.

The deposits revealed an extraordinarily long sequence of human occupation, with radiocarbon dates pushing back to approximately 16,700 years before present for the earliest Nachikufan I phase. This made Nachikufu not just locally significant but internationally important — a key reference site for understanding how Stone Age technology evolved in the woodland environments of south-central Africa.

Miller's doctoral research on the site, completed at Berkeley in 1969, established the terminology that archaeologists still use today. The Nachikufan tradition — divided into Nachikufan I, II, and III — became the standard framework for classifying Later Stone Age assemblages across northern Zambia and beyond.

The Rock Paintings

Nachikufu contains rock paintings in three distinct styles. The naturalistic paintings depict animals and humans in recognisable form — two elephants, an antelope, and human figures armed with bows — executed in red and, unusually for Zambia, in black pigment. The cave contains the only known black rock paintings in Zambia.

The schematic paintings are abstract geometric designs: grids, rectangles with vertical subdivisions, and parallel lines. The crude white paintings are the most recent, depicting animals and tools in greasy white paint.

A Living Sacred Place

The cave's significance extends beyond the Stone Age. During the turbulent nineteenth century, the Bisa and Lala peoples used Nachikufu as a refuge during raids by the Bemba and Ngoni. The Bisa believed the caves contained ancestral spirits who would protect them from their enemies — and when they emerged unharmed after the raids, it confirmed their faith.

This tradition continues: the Bisa still pay homage to their ancestral spirits at the site annually, and the western part of the cave complex serves as a burial ground for Bisa royal families. This is not merely an archaeological site but a living sacred place.

What You Will See

From the turnoff on the Great North Road, a track leads 2km to the cave site. The cave itself is a large rock shelter formed in a dramatic limestone kopje — the approach involves a moderate climb through rocky terrain to reach the main painted surfaces.

Inside, interpretation boards (somewhat weathered but legible) explain the archaeological sequence and rock art styles. The rock paintings are distributed across multiple panels — look carefully, as many are faded and require time for your eyes to adjust. The caretaker will point out details you'd otherwise miss, including the distinctive black paintings unique to this site.

Don't miss the climb to the top of the kopje. The panoramic views across the miombo woodland to distant mountain ranges are spectacular and help you understand why this location was so attractive to ancient peoples.

Getting There

From the south (Serenje), head north on the Great North Road (T2). The turnoff is signposted approximately 180km north of Serenje, on the left (west) side of the road. From the north (Mpika), head south — the turnoff is signposted approximately 56km south of Mpika, on the right (west) side.

Follow the dirt track for approximately 2km to the site. The road is generally passable by 2WD in dry season but may require higher clearance after rains.

Tips for Visitors

  • Allow at least an hour, more if you climb to the summit
  • Engage with the caretaker — he knows the site intimately and can point out details you'd miss
  • Look carefully at the paintings — many are faded and require time for your eyes to adjust
  • Never touch the rock art — oils from skin accelerate deterioration
  • Respect the sacred nature — the western caves are used for Bisa royal burials
  • Bring water, sun protection, and sturdy footwear for the rocky terrain

Suggested Itineraries
