Nayuma Museum

Preserving the heritage of Barotseland

The royal storehouse of one of Africa's oldest kingdoms: ceremonial drums, divination figurines, witchcraft objects, and the regalia of the Litunga — all packed into a modest museum beside the winter palace of the Lozi king. Small, eclectic, absolutely worth the drive from Mongu.

The Story

Nayuma means "storehouse" in Silozi, and that is exactly what this small museum is: the material memory of one of Africa's oldest surviving kingdoms, preserved in a modest building beside the Litunga's winter palace in Limulunga.

The Lozi people have lived on the Barotse floodplain for centuries, their lives shaped by the annual flooding of the Zambezi. When the rains come, the Litunga — the king — moves from his summer capital at Lealui to this palace in Limulunga on higher ground. This migration, the Kuomboka ceremony, is one of Zambia's great cultural spectacles: a royal procession by water, drums beating, the Litunga in the full regalia of a British admiral, thousands of people lining the route. The ceremony celebrates survival, continuity, and the unbroken authority of the Litunga through more than 400 years of history.

The museum was founded in the mid-1980s by His Majesty King Ilute Yeta IV (1924–2000), who reigned from 1977 until his death. The son of Yeta III and grandson of the great King Lewanika, Ilute Yeta was a diplomat before becoming Litunga — he served as High Commissioner to Botswana and was a member of UNIP's Central Committee. He understood both traditional authority and modern statecraft, and he recognized that Lozi material culture needed preservation. With funding from NORAD, he established Nayuma as a cultural centre managed by the Barotse Royal Establishment. Remembered as the most beloved Litunga of modern times, the museum remains his legacy.

What You Will See

The museum occupies a traditional-style building adjacent to the Litunga's palace. Outside you may see traditional instruments including a raised xylophone (silimba), its wooden keys resting on slats bound with rope.

Inside, the displays are organized thematically across several rooms. Lighting is natural — good for atmosphere, challenging for photography. Highlights include:

Royal regalia and portraits: Formal photographs and painted portraits of the Litunga in ceremonial dress, including the admiral's uniform worn during Kuomboka. There is also an architectural plan of the Litunga's palace at Lealui (Liangamba), showing the circular layout of the royal compound with its multiple courts, quarters, and ceremonial spaces.

Traditional crafts: Beautifully made snuff containers (mukuwa) in gourds and wood. A clay lion sculpture with hand-painted stripes, expressive rather than naturalistic. A "talking female figurine" — a carved wooden figure adorned with beads, with a cavity in the torso once used to hold substances for divination. These objects were consulted by traditional healers to communicate with ancestral spirits.

Witchcraft display: One of the most honest sections of the museum, explaining witchcraft as "the use of supernatural powers to attain a desired aim" — healing, protection, divination. The display categorizes witchcraft objects into offensive, defensive, divination, and regeneration types. It treats the subject anthropologically, acknowledging that these practices are widespread and meaningful.

Kuomboka documentation: Historical photographs of the annual ceremony — the royal drums, the Nalikwanda barge with its elephant figurehead, paddlers in traditional dress, crowds at the harbor. For those who cannot attend the ceremony itself (it happens once a year, date announced a few weeks in advance), this provides essential context.

Historical Significance

The Nayuma Museum matters not for any spectacular individual artifact, but for what it represents: a traditional authority preserving its own history on its own terms.

The Lozi kingdom is one of the few pre-colonial African states that maintained continuity through colonialism and independence. The Litunga retained symbolic authority and institutional independence. The Barotse Royal Establishment — which manages this museum — operates parallel to the Zambian state. The museum is therefore an assertion of cultural sovereignty: the Lozi telling their story, in their institution, with their resources.

This is not a museum of dead cultures but of a culture actively preserving itself. Kuomboka happens every year, traditional healing remains widespread, the Litunga is still revered. The current Litunga of Barotseland is His Majesty King Lubosi Imwiko II.

Getting There

The museum is in Limulunga, approximately 16km north of Mongu on the main road. The drive is straightforward on a paved road, and the site is signposted. The museum is adjacent to the Litunga's palace compound. Parking is available on site.

Minibuses run regularly between Mongu and Limulunga (approximately K5–10). Any taxi or tour operator in Mongu will know the location.

Tips for Your Visit

Call ahead to confirm opening hours, as the museum is a small operation and hours can be variable. Take your time with the displays — the museum is compact but densely packed with information. Pay particular attention to the explanatory text on the witchcraft display, which provides important cultural context.

If you have questions, ask the staff — they are knowledgeable about the collection and the history of Barotseland. Consider visiting during or around the Kuomboka ceremony (March/April, date announced in advance) to see the museum in context and experience the ceremony itself.

Suggested Itineraries

Limulunga Cultural Morning (2–3 hours): Nayuma Museum → walk around Limulunga village → view Litunga's palace compound from outside. Provides context for the Lozi kingdom and royal life.

Barotse Floodplain Sunset Drive (half-day): Morning at Nayuma Museum, then in late afternoon drive west on the new embanked road across the floodplain to Lealui. Cross the spectacular bridge over the Zambezi and watch the sunset over the floodplain — truly dramatic views of water, reeds, and distant palms. Best in flood season (March–June) when the plain is full of water.

Kuomboka Experience (2–3 days): Visit Nayuma Museum on day 1 to learn about the ceremony. Attend Kuomboka on day 2 (advance planning required, ceremony date announced a few weeks before). Follow the royal barge from Lealui to Limulunga. This is the definitive Western Province cultural experience.
