Mwela Rocks

Ancient rock art of Northern Zambia

Over 1,000 paintings spread across 100 square kilometres of granite outcrops — the largest concentration of rock art in southern Africa, hidden in the bush outside a town most travellers pass through without stopping. The Bemba still consider these caves sacred. UNESCO has taken notice. You should too.

The Story

The granite outcrops east of Kasama have drawn humans for as long as humans have existed in this region. The rounded boulders and overhanging shelters offered protection from rain and predators; the elevated positions provided views across the surrounding plateau; the smooth rock surfaces invited marking.

The Bemba who have lived here for generations call this place Mwela after the spirit they believe inhabits it. For them, certain caves remain sacred, used for rain-making ceremonies and communication with ancestors. For visitors, Mwela offers something increasingly rare: world-class rock art in an unmanicured landscape where the paintings emerge gradually as your eyes adjust, where a local guide's torch beam picks out images that have watched from the rock face for ten thousand years.

The artists responsible for these works are identified as the Batwa — the hunter-gatherer peoples who inhabited this region before the arrival of Bantu-speaking agriculturalists. These paintings predate the arrival of the Bemba and represent traditions that had likely already spanned thousands of years by the time the first farmers arrived.

Two Artistic Traditions

The paintings fall into two distinct traditions. The naturalistic tradition depicts recognisable animals — elephants, elands, bush pigs, lions — often with remarkable attention to movement and form. These images, executed in red ochre, show creatures in motion: running, grazing, turning.

The schematic tradition is more abstract: geometric patterns of dots arranged in lines, grids, and circles; fingerprint-like marks; ladder shapes; concentric rings. These enigmatic designs — sometimes running in parallel, sometimes converging in complex branching patterns — resist easy interpretation. Are they tallies? Star maps? Representations of trance experiences? The artists left no written explanation.

Contemporary research suggests that many rock art traditions had spiritual or shamanic dimensions — that the images functioned within complex belief systems involving altered states of consciousness, communication with the spirit world, and the mediation of relationships between humans, animals, and supernatural forces.

A Living Sacred Place

The Bemba integrated the rock shelters into their own spiritual landscape. The sites were protected by imichiliko (prohibitions) and imipamba (restrictions) — traditional laws governing behaviour, dress, and access. Only herbalists under spiritual possession could enter certain areas to gather medicinal plants. Rain-making ceremonies continue. Burial practices incorporate certain sites. The traditional authority of Senior Chief Mwamba extends over the land that holds the paintings.

In 2009, Mwela was inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage Tentative List, recognising it as one of Africa's most significant rock art concentrations. The nomination describes the site as having "outstanding universal value."

What You Will See

The main visitor area is reached from a signposted entrance off Mungwi Road, about 5km east of Kasama. A caretaker provides guided tours — essential, since many paintings are faded or positioned in locations an unaccompanied visitor would miss. The guide leads visitors through miombo woodland, climbing over granite boulders and ducking under overhangs to reach the painted surfaces.

Animal paintings: Elephants, elands, antelope, bush pigs, and lions executed in profile, often capturing movement. Preservation varies; some remain remarkably clear, while others require careful observation in good light.

Human figures: Elongated forms appear throughout, sometimes alone, sometimes interacting with animals. Some hold implements; others raise their arms in gestures whose meaning is lost.

Schematic designs: Lines of finger dots running across rock surfaces, circular designs near cave entrances, grid patterns and "ladder" shapes. Mwela's most distinctive feature.

The landscape: Massive boulders balance on narrow pedestals; overhangs create natural shelters; caves open into darkness. The geology that created this landscape also created the canvas on which the paintings were made.

Getting There

From Kasama, head east on Mungwi Road (towards Isoka). Pass Kasama General Hospital on your left. Continue approximately 2km beyond the hospital until you reach a signposted turn for "Mwela Rocks National Monument."

By taxi: K50–100 return from Kasama town centre including waiting time.

By minibus: Minibuses heading towards Mungwi, Mbala, or Nakonde pass the entrance.

Tips for Visitors

  • Hire a guide: Essential, not optional. Many paintings are faded or hidden; guides know where to look.
  • Morning or afternoon: Indirect light reveals paintings best. Avoid midday when harsh shadows obscure images.
  • Let your eyes adjust: Paintings that seem invisible at first often emerge after 30–60 seconds of patient looking.
  • Bring water: At least 1 litre per person — moderate walking but can be warm.
  • Good footwear: Comfortable shoes suitable for climbing over rocks.
  • Respect sacred sites: Some caves retain spiritual significance. Follow your guide's instructions.
  • Allow time: The main circuit takes 2–3 hours. Rock art viewing cannot be rushed.
