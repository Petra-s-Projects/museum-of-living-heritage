Chirundu Fossil Forest

Petrified remnants of ancient woodlands

One hundred and fifty million years ago, this was a temperate forest of giant conifers. Today, their petrified remains lie scattered across 43 square kilometres of mopane woodland — and through the villages of Chief Sikoongo's people, where Jurassic tree trunks serve as benches and gathering spots. Deep time, casually encountered.

The Fossils

During the Jurassic period, this corner of southern Zambia looked nothing like the hot, dry mopane woodland you see today. Giant coniferous trees — species now assigned to Dadyoxlon sp. and Rhexoxylon africanum — flourished in a temperate climate with distinct seasons. Then catastrophe struck: a massive flood felled the forest, burying the trees in sediment.

Over millions of years, silica-rich groundwater infiltrated the buried wood, replacing organic material molecule by molecule. The trees turned to stone, preserving their structure in extraordinary detail. Growth rings remain visible. Bark textures survive. Some trunks exceed ten metres in length and 1.2 metres in diameter.

The fossils lie within the Escarpment Grit Formation of the Upper Karoo Group — the same geological sequence that yields fossils across southern Africa and gives its name to South Africa's Karoo Basin. The Chirundu site is comparable to Arizona's famous Petrified Forest, though far less developed for tourism.

The Village

What makes Chirundu unusual is not the fossils themselves — petrified wood exists across southern Africa — but their setting. This is not a fenced reserve with interpretive panels and walking trails. The fossils lie scattered through active villages on Chief Sikoongo's customary land, where they have been incorporated into daily life.

Villagers use the petrified trunks as benches, boundary markers, and informal furniture. Children play among stones that predate the dinosaurs. It is heritage embedded in the everyday — Jurassic geology serving as a place to sit and chat in the afternoon shade.

Some fossils remain in situ, exactly where the trees fell 150 million years ago. Others have been moved by water action over geological time, scattered through streambeds and low-lying areas. The fragmentation visible in smaller pieces likely resulted from trunks colliding during ancient floods.

A Window into a Vanished World

One hundred and fifty million years ago, southern Africa's climate resembled modern temperate regions — cool enough for conifers, wet enough to support dense forest. The alternating coarse and fine layers in the surrounding sedimentary rock suggest seasonal variation, perhaps monsoon-like wet and dry cycles.

The transformation from temperate forest to hot mopane woodland reflects the profound climate shifts that have shaped Africa over geological time. The coniferous species that once dominated here are entirely absent from modern Zambia. Only their stone ghosts remain.

The site's inclusion on Zambia's UNESCO Tentative List recognises its scientific importance. The fossils provide evidence for understanding Gondwanan environments during the Jurassic period and complement similar sites in Zimbabwe, South Africa, and beyond.

What You Will See

This is not a developed tourist site. There are no facilities, no toilets, no refreshments. You are visiting a working village where fossils happen to lie scattered through the landscape. Behave as a guest: greet people, ask permission before photographing individuals, and tip the guide appropriately.

The fossils themselves are remarkable. Growth rings, bark patterns, and wood grain are clearly visible in the best specimens. The contrast between the ancient stone and the living village creates a unique atmosphere — deep time made tangible and domestic.

Getting There

The fossil forest lies just south of the main Lusaka–Chirundu road (T2), approximately 21 km west of Chirundu town and about 15 km before the junction with the Siavonga road. Look for a turnoff on your left if travelling from Lusaka. The site is accessible by 2WD in dry season.

The Guide

Mr Amos has been guiding visitors at the site for nearly two decades. He knows every significant specimen and can explain the geology in accessible terms. A donation is expected — this is his livelihood, and there is no formal entry fee. Ask at the village for the guide. He will find you.

Tips for Visitors

  • Bring water and sun protection — there are no facilities and limited shade
  • This is a working village, not a reserve — behave as a respectful guest
  • Ask permission before photographing villagers
  • Tip the guide generously — sustainable tourism means supporting local people
  • The site makes an ideal stop on the Lusaka–Lower Zambezi or Lake Kariba route
  • Best visited in dry season when access roads are more reliable
